🍳 St. Patrick’s Recipe Corner ☘️
🍞

Classic Irish Soda Bread

A traditional St. Patrick’s Day staple — no yeast, no waiting, just warmth
⏰
10 min
Prep
🔥
45 min
Bake
🍴
8
Servings
⭐
Easy
Level
☘️ Ingredients
  • 4 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1 tsp baking soda
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1¾ cups buttermilk
  • 2 tbsp sugar (optional)
  • 1 cup raisins (optional)
  • 2 tbsp butter, melted
  • 1 egg, lightly beaten
🍳 Directions
  1. Preheat oven to 375°F (190°C). Lightly grease a baking sheet or cast iron skillet.
  2. Mix dry: Whisk together flour, baking soda, salt, and sugar in a large bowl.
  3. Add wet: Make a well in the center. Pour in buttermilk, melted butter, and egg. Stir until just combined — don’t overmix! Fold in raisins if using.
  4. Shape: Turn dough onto a floured surface. Shape into a round loaf about 7 inches across. Place on baking sheet. Cut a deep X across the top (tradition says it lets the fairies out).
  5. Bake: 40–45 minutes until golden brown and hollow-sounding when tapped on the bottom.
  6. Serve: Let cool slightly. Slice thick, slather with butter, and enjoy with your St. Patrick’s Day meal!
💡 Pro tip: The X on top isn’t just decorative — it helps the heat penetrate the thick loaf evenly. And yes, Irish grandmas really do say it “lets the fairies out.” 🧙
📚 The Story of March 🌱
History • Science • Culture • Tradition
🌞
The Spring (Vernal) Equinox
Falling around March 20–21, this marks the official start of spring in the Northern Hemisphere. Day and night are nearly equal in length — a cosmic reset. After this, the days get longer and the world comes alive again.
⚔️
Named After Mars
March is named after Mars, the Roman god of war. It historically marked the beginning of the war season and was the first month of the ancient Roman calendar.
📡
The Ides of March
March 15, 44 B.C. — the day Julius Caesar was assassinated by members of the Roman Senate. Shakespeare made “Beware the Ides of March” legendary.

Women’s History Month
The entire month honors the contributions of women to history and society. Started as a week-long celebration in 1978, expanded to a full month in 1987.
⏰
Daylight Saving Time
Clocks “spring forward” one hour, giving us more evening daylight. Originally proposed by Benjamin Franklin in 1784 — as a joke.
🌱
Nature & Gardening
Known for “in like a lion, out like a lamb” weather. March 12 is National Plant a Flower Day — the perfect time to start planting!
💡 Fun Fact: Pi Day (3/14) is also Albert Einstein’s birthday. He was born in 1879 in Ulm, Germany. Math + genius = same day. 💡
🧠 March Trivia ❓
Q: What does the name “March” come from?
🌟 Mars, the Roman god of war. In the ancient Roman calendar, March (Martius) was the first month of the year!
Q: What is the only NCAA tournament seed to have NEVER won a championship?
🏀 A #9 seed has never won the NCAA tournament. The lowest seed to win it all was #8 Villanova in 1985.
Q: What famous phrase is associated with March?
🦁 “In like a lion, out like a lamb” — referring to March starting with harsh weather and ending mild!
Q: How many gallons of green dye does Chicago use to dye their river for St. Patrick’s Day?
☘️ About 40 pounds of vegetable-based dye! The tradition started in 1962 and the river stays green for about 5 hours.
